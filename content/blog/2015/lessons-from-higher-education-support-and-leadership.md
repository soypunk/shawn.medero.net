If someone were to ask me today:

_"Shawn, you've been working to support Higher Education for nearly a decade - what do I need to know to jump into this sector?"_

Two recently written articles do the job for me so I'll summarize and let this URL stand as my answer.

First, from the [Leadership Foundation for Higher Education][1] comes [this research on "micropolitics"][2]:

> "Micropolitics is the subtle exercise of power through influence, social skills and informal activity. It's the habitual strategies that are little discussed – the choices over what to communicate and to whom, what to reveal or conceal, how structures, information and meetings are managed. It is what happens in the wings and backstage when nobody's looking."

Working as a staff member in support of Higher Education you will often find yourself in roles where you have the appearance (or perhaps expectation of) some autonomy but the reality is more subtle than that.

> "It was seen to be especially necessary in higher education where staff expect a certain level of autonomy, meaning that overt displays of direct power from authority figures tend to be viewed as inappropriate. Micropolitics is the essential alternate way of getting things done."

Spot on. Coming from several jobs in the corporate sector and startups I was underprepared for this world and was lucky to have someone directly mentor me who was highly skilled at it. The politics are rarely transparent and extremely tricky to navigate... without a guide you will not summit the mountain.

Related to these issues is also a more general purpose skill but one that helps mitigate the risks of aforementioned politicking. It is a [study by David Deming from the National Bureau of Economic Research][3] and summarized in the [New York Times][4]:

> "...skills like cooperation, empathy and flexibility have become increasingly vital in modern-day work. Occupations that require strong social skills have grown much more than others since 1980, according to new research."

I've worked with countless people in this world who simply "do not play well others" and what happens is people will work around you. The political environment incentivizes those that learn how to identify people that you can't horse-trade with and adjust your systematic path to meet your desired outcome.

Micropolitics also need microadjusments in communication, note this quote from Professor Deming:

> "As I’m speaking with you, I need to think about what’s going on in your head — ‘Is she bored? Am I giving her too much information?’ — and I have to adjust my behavior all the time"

If you find yourself in a Higher Education Leadership role then I can only emphasize how important ["Management by wandering around"][5] will be for you. It works much like the Pre-School teacher wandering between the open "centers" students gravitate towards that represent their interest and the teacher subtlety turning their play into learning. Likewise, you have to coach & mentor people in the moment turning so many communications into teachable lessons learned for your staff.
